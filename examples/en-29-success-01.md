# Pattern 29: False Nuance (Retroactive Reframing) — Success

## Input Text

> A four-day workweek raises employee satisfaction. Actually, it's more nuanced than that — a four-day week can improve satisfaction in certain contexts while presenting challenges in others, and the net effect depends on organizational culture and individual work styles.

## Expected Output

> A four-day workweek raises employee satisfaction. The effect splits by role, though: Microsoft Japan's trial reported a 40% productivity gain, while in coverage-critical jobs the remaining staff just absorbed the missing shifts and burned out.

## Applied Pattern

- Pattern 29 (False Nuance): Immediately after the opening claim, the watch phrase "Actually, it's more nuanced than that" reintroduces the same claim, and the reframing sentence ("can improve... while presenting challenges... depends on culture and work styles") adds no new information — it dilutes the first sentence into an on-the-other-hand restatement.

## Judgment

**Success** — The trigger condition is met exactly. The reframing sentence offers only empty hedges ("in certain contexts," "depends on organizational culture and individual work styles") with no new evidence and no shift in conclusion, manufacturing an illusion of depth. The rewrite removes the false-nuance sentence and keeps the opening claim intact, then replaces the cosmetic qualification with a genuine one backed by concrete evidence (Microsoft Japan's 40% versus burnout in coverage-critical roles). Per the preservation note, the primary claim is retained rather than deleted.
